Top and bottom signals

The on-chain and moving-average tools that have marked past cycle extremes. They mark zones, not exact days, and their thresholds keep going quiet as each cycle swings less than the last. Read them together, never alone.

Realized price: are we above or below what the average holder paid

The realized price is roughly the average price every coin last moved at, so it is a fair proxy for what the typical holder paid. Price above the line means the average holder is in profit. Every one of the last four cycle bottoms printed below it.

Price What the average holder paid

The undercut shrinks every cycle (58, 44, 31, 25 percent below the line). That is the same maturation you see everywhere in Bitcoin: smaller swings each time. The honest caveat is that price has spent long stretches below this line without it being the bottom, so it marks a value zone, not a day.

MVRV: price versus the market's cost basis

MVRV is price divided by that same average cost basis. Below 1 means the average holder is underwater, which has clustered around bottoms. High readings have marked froth, though the level that counts as high keeps falling.

Mayer Multiple: price versus the 200-day average

Price divided by its 200-day moving average. Historically, readings under 1 have been good long-term value and readings above about 2.4 have been overheated. A simple, durable temperature gauge.

Puell Multiple: how well miners are being paid

Daily miner income in dollars divided by its own one-year average. Low readings mean miners are earning far less than usual, which has lined up with bottoms. High readings mean unusually rich pay, which has lined up with tops.

Pi Cycle Top: the classic top-catcher

When the 111-day average crosses above twice the 350-day average, it has landed within days of the 2013, 2017 and 2021 tops. Notice it did not fire in 2025. The swings are shrinking, so this tool is going quiet.

The 200-week moving average: Bitcoin's long-term floor

The average price over the last 200 weeks. Bitcoin has only closed below it at the deepest points of past bear markets, so it acts as a rising floor. How far price sits above it tells you how stretched or how cheap the market is.

Macro and cross-asset

Does Bitcoin follow the money supply, the business cycle, or the stock market? These are the charts behind those debates. Two of them mostly say no, and being honest about that is the point.

M2 money supply versus Bitcoin

The liquidity story. When central banks expand the money supply (M2), the argument goes, Bitcoin rises. Over the long run the two do broadly move together, but the link is loose and it breaks for months at a time.

Bitcoin (log, left) US M2 money supply (right)

Estimated miner production cost

Roughly what it costs in electricity to mine one Bitcoin, estimated from network hashrate and the daily coin issuance. Miners tend to defend this level because selling below it for long is unsustainable, so it has acted as a soft floor. This is a model, not a measured number, so treat it as a band.

Price Estimated electricity cost Cost range

The estimate scales two assumptions: how efficient the average miner is (joules per terahash) and what they pay for power (dollars per kilowatt-hour). The shaded band walks both to their optimistic and pessimistic ends, so the real cost almost certainly sits inside it.

PMI versus Bitcoin: the business cycle test

The ISM Manufacturing PMI is the classic read on whether the economy is expanding (above 50) or contracting (below 50). If Bitcoin were a bet on growth, its cycles would track it. They do not. Bitcoin ran a full bull market straight through a long PMI contraction.

Bitcoin (log, left) ISM Manufacturing PMI (right)

S&P 500 correlation: is Bitcoin just tech stocks

You often hear that Bitcoin trades in lockstep with the stock market. Here is the year-by-year correlation of daily moves. It swings from strongly positive to negative and averages far below the headline number people quote. The relationship is real in stress moments and mostly noise the rest of the time.

Next halving countdown

Every 210,000 blocks, roughly every four years, the new supply of Bitcoin is cut in half. It is the metronome the whole cycle runs on.

How to read these charts

No single indicator calls a top or a bottom. Each one here has been early, late, or flat-out wrong at least once. What has held up is reading them as a group and asking whether they agree. When the average holder is underwater, miners are barely paid, and price is pressing on its 200-week floor all at the same time, that is the kind of cheap that has mattered before.

The other honest theme is that the swings are shrinking. The old top signals were calibrated on cycles that ran ten times hotter than today. As Bitcoin matures, those thresholds fire later or not at all, while the timing on the halving schedule has held. That is the whole idea behind the Satoshi Clock.

Where the numbers come from

Price and the on-chain series come from Coin Metrics community data. The money supply is US M2 from the Federal Reserve. The PMI is the ISM Manufacturing index. The S&P 500 is public market data. The miner cost is our own estimate with its assumptions shown on the chart. Everything updates automatically. This is education, not financial advice.
